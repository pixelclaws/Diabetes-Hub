Glossary | What do you know about diabetes?
Diabetes
In general terms, diabetes refers to the condition of suffering from persistent hyperglycaemia. A patient with diabetic syndrome is defined as diabetic if their
> HbA1c :
48 mmol/mol (6.5%) or more.
> Fasting Plasma Glucose :
7.0 mmol/L or more.
> Plasma Glucose :
11.1 mmol/L or more.
Source: National Institue of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases
Diabetes is a disease that occurs when your blood glucose, also called blood sugar, is too high. Blood glucose is your main source of energy and comes from the food you eat. Insulin, a hormone made by the pancreas, helps glucose from food get into your cells to be used for energy. Sometimes your body doesn’t make enough—or any—insulin or doesn’t use insulin well. Glucose then stays in your blood and doesn’t reach your cells.
Over time, having too much glucose in your blood can cause health problems. Although diabetes has no cure, you can take steps to manage your diabetes and stay healthy.
Sometimes people call diabetes “a touch of sugar” or “borderline diabetes.” These terms suggest that someone doesn’t really have diabetes or has a less serious case, but every case of diabetes is serious.
Who is eligible for surgical therapy?

Patients with BMI > 40, patients with BMI > 35 + significant comorbidities, or patients with BMI > 30 + T2DM (of less than 10 years duration).

73% of surgery achieves remission, whilst only 13% of conventional therapy achieves remission.

JAMA, 2008
Process of treating a diagnosed diabetic patient:

  1. Lifestyle intervention (12 months)
  2. Referred to hospital → specialist weight management service (could take up to 6 months)
  3. Surgery

What is GLP-1?

GLP-1 is secreted upon the ingestion of food by beta cells, alpha cells, the liver, the stomach and the brain to promote satiety and reduce appetite.

Can the gut regulate glucose levels?

Yes. In gastric bypass patients, the 1st part of the small intestine (the part that's bypassed) is enlarged and could be measured by PET (PET measures glucose uptake of a specific organ). This part of the gut starts using glucose and removing it from the circulation for its own energy demands and eventually becomes a second weight loss independent mechanism of gastric bypass surgeries. However, this does not happen to sleeve or band patients.

NICE Guidances for diagnosis (2021)
If patient was symptomatic, a single abnormal HbA1c or fasting plasma glucose level can be used, although repeat testing is sensible to confirm the diagnosis.
If patient was asymptomatic, it is advised not to diagnose diabetes on the basis of a single abnormal HbA1c or plasma glucose result (as a single test can only be viewed as a screening test). Proceed to arrange repeat testing, preferably with the same test, to confirm the diagnosis.
